\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================

Innovation in frontier technologies rarely happens in isolation.
It tends to cluster geographically, to follow temporal sequences,
and to leave traces across multiple types of knowledge artifact:
academic papers, patents, and — more recently — open-science platforms
like the iGEM (International Genetically Engineered Machine) student
competition \citep{igem}.

This paper asks a specific question: within synthetic biology,
do student projects, academic publications, and patents form
\emph{semantically related local innovation trajectories}?
The working hypothesis is that innovation in synthetic biology
may follow a local path in which:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item student projects introduce early ideas,
  \item academic publications develop and refine them,
  \item and patents reflect downstream application or commercial translation.
\end{enumerate}

We do not claim to establish causality.
We use language of \emph{semantic relatedness}, \emph{association},
\emph{cluster overlap}, and \emph{plausible temporal sequence}.

The primary case study is \textbf{carbon capture in synthetic biology}:
the engineering of microorganisms to fix CO\textsubscript{2} or produce
carbon-neutral fuels. This subfield is large enough to find examples
across all three artifact types and is directly relevant to planetary
sustainability.

The analysis combines three data sources:
(1) papers from OpenAlex \citep{priem2022openalex},
(2) patents from Lens.org,
and (3) iGEM project and parts data from the iGEM Registry.
All artifacts are encoded with a shared sentence-transformer embedding
\citep{reimers2019sentencebert} and projected to a shared semantic space
using UMAP \citep{mcinnes2018umap}.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Embedding}

Each artifact's title and abstract were concatenated and encoded with
\texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2}, a sentence-transformer model that produces
384-dimensional dense vectors capturing semantic meaning
\citep{reimers2019sentencebert}.

\subsection{Dimensionality reduction}

Embeddings were projected to 2D with UMAP using the cosine distance metric,
$n\_\text{neighbors} = 15$, and $\text{min\_dist} = 0.1$
\citep{mcinnes2018umap}.

\subsection{Clustering}

Clusters were identified with HDBSCAN \citep{campello2013hdbscan} applied
to the UMAP-reduced coordinates, with $\text{min\_cluster\_size} = 10$.

\subsection{Case study identification}

Artifacts were tagged as belonging to the carbon-capture case study if
their text contained at least one of a predefined keyword list
(carbon capture, carbon sequestration, CO\textsubscript{2} fixation, etc.).

\subsection{Geocoding}

Institution locations were geocoded to city-level latitude/longitude
coordinates using Nominatim (OpenStreetMap).

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
% ============================================================

Several limitations should be noted.
First, keyword-based corpus construction may miss relevant work using
different terminology and may include tangentially relevant work.
Second, geocoding is approximate and some institution locations are missing.
Third, iGEM project descriptions vary in length and quality.
Fourth, we infer semantic relatedness from co-occurrence in embedding space,
not from direct citation links.
