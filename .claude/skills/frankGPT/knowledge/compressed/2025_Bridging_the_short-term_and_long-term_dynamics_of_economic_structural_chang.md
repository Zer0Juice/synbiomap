Bridging the short-term and long-term

dynamics of economic structural change

James McNerney 1,2,3 , Yang Li 1,4, Andres Gomez-Lievano 1,5 &
Frank Neffke 1,2

Received: 19 July 2022

Accepted: 6 October 2025

Economic development hinges on structural change, that is, transformations
in what an economy produces. The field of economic complexity has investigated
this process through two related but distinct branches: one studying
how economies diversify, the other how the complexity of an economy is
reflected in its output. However, a formal connection between these approaches,
and their relationship to classic accounts of structural transformation
(for example, from agriculture to manufacturing), remains unclear. Here we
introduce a simple dynamical model that links these perspectives through one
core idea: economies diversify preferentially into activities related to those
they already do. Studying this model yields three main results: It generates
quantities resembling economic complexity metrics, suggests these metrics
summarize long-term structural change rather than directly infer an economy’s
complexity, and reproduces stylized facts of development. Our framework
formally connects the field’s conceptual strands, bridges short and long
timescales of change, and adds granularity to classic descriptions of
development.

Diversity and composition of exports baskets over time

Income increases with b, diversity with A. A higher value of the
compositional coordinate b for a country’s export basket is associated
with significantly higher Gross Domestic Product (GDP) per capita
(Fig. 3a). For example, holding A fixed at 0.5, an increase in b from −0.5
to +0.5 is associated with an increase in a country’s per capita income
from about $1500 to $30,000 (2010 US dollars at Purchasing Power
Parity or PPP). In contrast, the association between a country’s average
ability A and its income when holding the value of b fixed is weak
at best.

While RCAs are a widely used standard, they do have potential side
effects. As one example, an increase in a location’s output of one product
may decrease its RCA in other products. While this effect tends to be
small, it can be exacerbated when RCAs are binarized around a threshold
(typically Rcp = 1). Binarization is frequently done to generate a simplified
picture of what products an economy exports competitively, and to
curtail outlier effects from RCAs, which tend to be heavily skewed. In this
case, a product beginning with an RCA just above the threshold may
realize a new RCA below the threshold, with the counter-intuitive result
that the economy appears to ‘lose’ its ability to effectively export a
product (even though its exports of the product have not changed).
(This effect does not impact our findings. We study a continuous-time
model where elements of R(t) are not binarized and evolve continuously.
Binarized RCAs could be used in computing the proximity matrix Φ,
where we tried three methods used in the literature (Supplementary
Note 6). Two of these, ΦP and ΦM, use thresholded RCAs while the third,
ΦC
, does not. We do not find large differences in our results, whichever
matrix is used. For an extensive empirical investigation of different
specifications of the proximity matrix, see Li & Neffke 202465).

Like the conventional ECI (Eq. (8)), the ECI* is an average of PCI*s. The
conventional ECI uses uniform weights for all of the nonzero elements
of the binarized matrix M. The ECI*, in contrast, uses non-uniform
weights based on the elements of the unbinarized matrix R, and
adjusted by the ergodic probabilities π. (See Supplementary Note 14
for an interpretation and potential benefits of these weights).

Description of data

We use UN Comtrade data47 available at Harvard Dataverse62. Trade
data are reported twice: once as exports by the exporting country and
once as imports by the importing country. The data cleaning corrects
trade flows to increase the consistency between importer and exporter
records of the same flow, and further corrects reported values using an
index of reliability based on the consistency of reported values over
time. The data provide total export volumes of products for 249
countries over the period 1962–2018 (11,544 export baskets observed
across all regions and years). We analyze these data at the 3-digit SITC
product level (235 product categories).

Other quantities have also been used to attempt to avoid some of
the drawbacks of RCAs. For example, Revealed per Capita Advantage
(RpCA) replaces the total export output of a country with population66,
which removes the impact of changes in output of one good on others.
As another approach, Brummitt et al.35 use a regression model to
construct a country’s expected export volumes in each good. These
alternatives represent other ways to put export volumes on a common
scale. Here, we deliberately use the conventional RCA, since this is
what is most frequently used in analyses of the PoR, but the ideal
approach remains debated63.
